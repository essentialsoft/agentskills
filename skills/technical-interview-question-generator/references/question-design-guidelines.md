# Question Design Guidelines

Use these rules when building questions for the interview packet.

## Core Principles

- Prefer resume-verification over generic trivia.
- Ask questions that reveal actual decision-making, execution, and depth.
- Favor concrete examples, tradeoffs, failures, recovery, and measurable outcomes.
- Match question difficulty to the target role and stage.
- Avoid questions that reward memorization more than judgment.

## Good Question Types

Use a balanced mix of:

- experience validation questions
- debugging or problem-isolation questions
- architecture and tradeoff questions
- prioritization and execution questions
- collaboration and stakeholder-management questions
- domain-specific reasoning questions when context supports them

## What Strong Questions Do

Strong questions should:

- test a specific claim from the resume or role expectations
- have a clear reason for being asked
- allow the interviewer to distinguish shallow familiarity from ownership
- create room for follow-up depth
- reveal how the candidate thinks under realistic constraints

## What to Avoid

- trivia that can be answered from rote memory
- questions disconnected from the role or project
- vague prompts with no scoring value
- compound questions that test too many things at once
- gotcha questions meant to embarrass the candidate

## Follow-Up Rules

Add follow-up prompts when needed to test depth:

- ask for a specific example
- ask what tradeoffs were considered
- ask what failed and how it was corrected
- ask how success was measured
- ask what the candidate would do differently now

## Evaluator Guidance Rules

For every question, include:

- why the question matters
- what strong answers should contain
- which red flags indicate risk

Do not omit `What you want to hear` or `Red flags` for any question in the final output.

Red flags should point to hiring risk such as lack of ownership, weak reasoning, poor communication, or inability to connect work to outcomes.
