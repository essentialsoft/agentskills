# ESI Career Guides Reference

Use this reference when the user provides ESI Career Guides materials or when the target role is tied to ESI Career Guides work.

## Purpose

Translate ESI Career Guides context into interview focus areas that are specific to the actual work instead of generating generic technical questions.

## How to Use ESI Career Guides Context

Extract and apply the following from the provided materials when available:

- target user groups
- product or program goals
- domain terminology that the candidate should understand
- workflow complexity and cross-functional dependencies
- delivery risks, constraints, or operational expectations
- evidence of what success looks like in the role

## What to Derive for the Interview Packet

From ESI Career Guides context, derive:

- which competencies matter most for this role
- which interview areas deserve the highest weight in the rating matrix
- which project-specific questions should replace generic questions
- which domain assumptions the candidate should be able to reason about

## Recommended Focus Areas

Consider these areas when the supplied ESI materials support them:

- product and program understanding
- stakeholder communication and cross-functional alignment
- technical fluency with the systems relevant to the role
- prioritization under ambiguity
- execution discipline and delivery ownership
- domain judgment tied to ESI Career Guides outcomes

## Question Framing Rules

- Prefer questions grounded in the provided ESI materials.
- Ask how the candidate would operate in the real project context, not in a generic hypothetical company.
- Use the materials to explain why each area matters in the rating matrix and focus-area rationale.
- Do not invent ESI requirements or business rules that are not present in the supplied context.

## Evidence Signals to Look For

Strong answers often include:

- concrete examples tied to similar work
- clear tradeoff reasoning
- awareness of stakeholder and delivery constraints
- evidence of measurable outcomes
- ability to connect technical choices to user or business impact

Red flags often include:

- generic answers that ignore the supplied context
- inability to connect prior experience to the role's actual work
- shallow use of domain terms without operational understanding
- no clear ownership examples
- weak reasoning about tradeoffs, risk, or execution
